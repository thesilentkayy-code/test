Following Jesus's resurrection, the Christian movement began in Jerusalem. The missionary journeys of apostles, particularly Paul, were instrumental in spreading the faith beyond its Jewish origins to Gentile (non-Jewish) communities across the Roman Empire, establishing churches in major urban centers.

Challenges: Persecution & Heresy

Early Christians faced both external threats from Roman persecution and internal challenges from theological disputes (heresies). In response, leaders called ecumenical councils to define and defend "orthodox" (correct) doctrine, solidifying the faith's core beliefs against differing interpretations.

Key Church Fathers

These influential theologians and leaders shaped the intellectual and doctrinal foundations of early Christianity through their writings and defenses of the faith.

  • Irenaeus (c. 130-202): Argued against Gnosticism, emphasizing apostolic tradition and the authority of the Gospels.
  • Origen (c. 185-254): A prolific scholar and writer from Alexandria who developed early systematic theology and biblical interpretation methods.
  • Augustine (354-430): One of the most important figures in Western Christianity, whose writings on sin, grace, and the church profoundly influenced theology for centuries.

Month 3: The Middle Ages and the Great Schism

East vs. West: The Path to Schism

Over centuries, cultural, political, and theological differences grew between the Latin-speaking West (centered in Rome) and the Greek-speaking East (centered in Constantinople). These tensions culminated in the Great Schism of 1054, formally dividing the church into the Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox branches.

Roman Catholic (West)

  • Headed by the Pope of Rome
  • Theology dominated by Latin
  • Accepted the *filioque* clause in the creed
  • Emphasized papal authority

Eastern Orthodox (East)

  • Led by the Patriarch of Constantinople
  • Theology developed in Greek
  • Rejected the *filioque* clause
  • Emphasized conciliar authority (councils)

Key Medieval Movements

The Middle Ages saw the rise of influential movements that shaped Christian culture, learning, and interaction with the wider world.

  • Monasticism: Monks and nuns in orders like the Benedictines preserved knowledge, developed agriculture, and provided spiritual centers.
  • Scholasticism: Theologians like Thomas Aquinas used rigorous logic and philosophy to systematically explain Christian doctrine.
  • The Crusades: A series of military campaigns aimed at reclaiming the Holy Land, with profound and often violent consequences for Christian-Muslim relations.

Month 4: The Reformation and Protestantism

Key Tenets of the Reformation

The Protestant Reformation was a major 16th-century movement that challenged the authority of the Roman Catholic Church. It resulted in the creation of Protestant churches and was founded on key theological principles that emphasized direct access to God and the authority of the Bible.

Major Branches of Protestantism

The Reformation wasn't a single movement but gave rise to several distinct traditions, each with its own theological emphasis and key leaders.

Lutheranism

Started by Martin Luther in Germany, emphasizing justification by faith alone.

Calvinism (Reformed)

Led by John Calvin in Switzerland, focusing on God's sovereignty and predestination.

Anglicanism

Established in England under King Henry VIII, blending Catholic and Protestant elements.

Anabaptism

The "radical" wing, advocating for adult baptism and separation of church and state.

Key Modern Movements

The modern era has been characterized by dynamic new movements that have reshaped the Christian landscape and fueled its global expansion.

  • Evangelicalism: Arising from the revival movements of the 18th century, it emphasizes personal conversion, biblical authority, and missionary work.
  • Pentecostalism: Beginning in the early 20th century, this is one of the fastest-growing segments, focusing on the direct experience of the Holy Spirit and spiritual gifts.

Christianity and the Enlightenment

The Age of Enlightenment in the 17th and 18th centuries posed significant challenges to traditional Christian thought by emphasizing reason, science, and individualism. This led to the development of liberal theology, biblical criticism, and new ways of reconciling faith with modern knowledge.
